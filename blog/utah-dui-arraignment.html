What Happens at a DUI Arraignment in Utah?

Most people charged with a DUI in Utah have never set foot in a courtroom. The arraignment is your first appearance — and for many defendants, it's the moment where the outcome of their case begins to take shape. Here is exactly what to expect, and what you should do before you get there.

What Happens Before the Arraignment

After a DUI arrest in Utah, you will typically go through booking at a county jail or police station. You may be released the same night on bail or on your own recognizance, or you may be held until a bail hearing. Either way, you will be given a date for your arraignment.

If you were held in custody, Utah law requires your arraignment to happen within 72 hours. If you were released, expect your first court date within a few weeks — you will receive a notice by mail with the exact date, time, and courtroom.

This window between arrest and arraignment is the most important time to act. Two separate proceedings are now running simultaneously: your criminal case in court, and a civil driver's license suspension process through the Utah Driver License Division (DLD). They are independent of each other, and missing a deadline in one does not affect the other — but missing the DLD deadline has consequences that are very difficult to undo.

What to Expect in the Courtroom

The arraignment itself is typically brief — often under ten minutes. A judge will read the charges against you formally, confirm that you understand them, and ask how you plead. You are not there to argue facts or present evidence. Nothing about the strength of the case is decided at this hearing.

The courtroom will likely have other defendants with scheduled hearings at the same time. This is normal. You will wait until your name is called.

What you wear matters. Courts notice. Dress as you would for a job interview — not a funeral, but not casual either. First impressions with judges are not meaningless.

The arraignment is not the moment to fight your case. It is the moment to protect your options — and that distinction is everything.

If you have an attorney, they will likely speak on your behalf for most of the hearing. If you do not, the judge will address you directly. You will be asked a few standard questions to confirm you understand the charges and the process. Then you will enter your plea.

Your Three Plea Options

At arraignment, you have three choices:

  • Not guilty — You are contesting the charge. This is the correct plea in the vast majority of cases, regardless of what happened that night.
  • Guilty — You are admitting to the charge and accepting whatever sentence follows. Doing this at arraignment, without negotiation or review of the evidence, is almost always a mistake.
  • No contest (nolo contendere) — You are not admitting guilt but are accepting the punishment. This is sometimes used to avoid the plea being used against you in a civil case. It is rarely the right choice at arraignment.

Enter a not guilty plea. Full stop.

A not guilty plea does not mean the case goes to trial. It simply preserves your ability to negotiate, review the evidence, file motions, and pursue a better outcome than what you would receive by walking into court and immediately accepting the state's version of events. Prosecutors know this. Judges know this. A not guilty plea at arraignment is standard — it signals nothing about your actual guilt or innocence.

Time-Sensitive

Your driver's license deadline may already be running.

Utah's DLD gives you 29 days from your arrest to request a hearing — after that, the right to contest your suspension is gone. Most people don't know about this until it's too late.

What Happens to Your Driver's License

This is where many people get blindsided. Your criminal case and your driver's license are handled by completely separate processes in Utah.

The Utah Driver License Division will automatically suspend your license beginning 29 days after your DUI arrest — unless you request a hearing within that window. This is an administrative process with the DLD, not a court proceeding. Missing the 29-day deadline eliminates your right to contest the suspension.

If you request a hearing in time, the suspension is stayed while your case is pending. You keep your license — and driving privileges — until the hearing is decided. That alone can be worth months of full driving ability while your criminal case works through the system.

An attorney who handles DUI cases regularly will file the DLD hearing request as one of the first things they do. If you are handling this yourself, call the DLD immediately — do not wait for the arraignment.

Should You Have an Attorney at Arraignment?

Yes — or at minimum, consult one before. The arraignment is where choices get made that affect everything that follows. The most valuable thing an attorney does at this stage is not what they say in court — it is what they do in the days leading up to it: filing for the DLD hearing, reviewing the police report and dashcam footage, identifying any issues with the stop or the test procedure, and advising you on what plea to enter and why.

If you cannot retain an attorney before your arraignment date, you have the right to request a continuance — a postponement — to obtain counsel. Courts grant these regularly for first appearances. Use it if you need it.

You also have the right to a court-appointed public defender if you cannot afford private representation. Ask the court at arraignment if that is your situation.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Pleading guilty at arraignment to "get it over with." You have not seen the evidence. You do not know what defenses exist. You do not know what the prosecutor was prepared to offer.
  • Missing the 29-day DLD deadline. By the time most people think about their license, the window has already closed.
  • Talking to police or prosecutors without an attorney. Anything you say about the incident can be used to build the case against you.
  • Assuming the BAC result is unbeatable. Test equipment must be properly maintained and calibrated. Officers must follow specific procedures. Errors in any of these create legitimate grounds to challenge results.
  • Waiting to hire an attorney. The earlier a defense attorney gets involved, the more options exist. Evidence can be lost. Witnesses' memories fade. Deadlines expire.

Frequently Asked Questions

Do I need an attorney at my DUI arraignment in Utah?

Yes — or at minimum, consult one before. The arraignment is where you enter your plea, and entering a guilty plea without speaking to a defense attorney first is one of the most costly mistakes DUI defendants make. Even a brief consultation can change the outcome of your case.

What happens to my driver's license after a DUI arrest in Utah?

Utah's Driver License Division (DLD) will automatically suspend your license 29 days after your arrest unless you request a hearing within that window. This hearing is separate from your criminal case. Missing the deadline means you lose your right to contest the suspension — which is why acting immediately after arrest matters.

What plea should I enter at my Utah DUI arraignment?

In almost every case, you should enter a not guilty plea at arraignment. This preserves all of your options — it does not mean you plan to take the case to trial. It simply keeps the door open to negotiate, challenge evidence, or reach a better outcome than what you would get by pleading guilty on day one.

How long after a DUI arrest is the arraignment in Utah?

If you were held in custody, your arraignment must happen within 72 hours of arrest. If you were released with a citation or on bail, it typically occurs within a few weeks. You will receive a notice with the date, time, and courtroom.
