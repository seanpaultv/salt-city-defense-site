What Is a Preliminary Hearing in Utah — and Why It Can Win Your Case

Most people charged with a felony in Utah have never heard of a preliminary hearing before their arrest. By the time they understand what one is, they've already waived it — often in exchange for a plea offer they didn't fully evaluate. That's a costly mistake. For felony defendants in Utah, the preliminary hearing is frequently the most important pretrial opportunity in the entire case, and one of the few moments where the defense holds real leverage over the prosecution.

What Is a Preliminary Hearing?

A preliminary hearing is a pretrial proceeding in which the prosecution must present enough evidence to establish probable cause that a crime was committed and that the defendant committed it. It is not a trial. The burden of proof is much lower than "beyond a reasonable doubt" — but it is still a real legal standard, and the state must actually meet it with witnesses and evidence presented to a judge.

In Utah, if the judge finds that probable cause has not been established, the charges are dismissed. If probable cause is found, the case is "bound over" to district court, where it will proceed toward trial. Either way, the preliminary hearing is a critical checkpoint — and the defense has the right to participate actively.

Think of it as the state's first public audition. The prosecutor must put a witness on the stand, swear them in, and have them explain what they saw or know. The defense attorney then gets to cross-examine that witness — under oath, on the record. Everything said at that hearing can and will be used later. That's what makes the preliminary hearing so valuable, even when charges are not dismissed outright.

Who Gets a Preliminary Hearing in Utah?

Under Utah law, defendants charged with felony offenses have a right to a preliminary hearing. Misdemeanor cases do not go through this process — they proceed on a different track through justice court or district court depending on the charge.

For felony charges, the preliminary hearing is typically scheduled within a few weeks of the initial appearance, though the timing can be extended by agreement or court scheduling. If you are held in custody, the law requires the hearing to be held within a reasonable time — delays beyond 30 days without good cause can themselves be challenged.

Important: You have the right to a preliminary hearing, but you can also waive it. Prosecutors routinely offer plea deals or other incentives in exchange for a waiver. Before you agree to waive this right, you need to understand exactly what you are giving up — and whether the deal being offered is actually worth it.

What Happens at a Utah Preliminary Hearing?

The prosecutor calls witnesses — typically law enforcement officers, alleged victims, or forensic experts — and asks them questions that walk through the basic facts of the case. The goal is to establish, in broad strokes, that a crime occurred and that this defendant was responsible.

The evidentiary rules at a preliminary hearing are relaxed compared to trial. Hearsay is often admissible. The judge does not weigh credibility the same way a jury would. The bar for "probable cause" is intentionally lower than the trial standard — the question is not whether the defendant is guilty, but whether there is enough evidence to justify making them stand trial.

What this means in practice: the state does not have to put on its best case. Prosecutors know they only need to clear a low bar. But that also means they can't hide behind vague summaries — they have to produce actual witnesses who testify under oath, and those witnesses are subject to cross-examination.

The preliminary hearing is not a mini-trial. But it is often the only time before trial that you get a witness under oath, on the record, before they've been coached through months of preparation.

Why Cross-Examination at the Prelim Matters

The most powerful aspect of a preliminary hearing is the right to cross-examine. Here's why that matters more than most defendants realize:

Locking in testimony

Every word a witness says at the preliminary hearing is transcribed and becomes part of the official record. If that witness later changes their story at trial — claims they don't remember something, contradicts an earlier statement, or suddenly recalls a new detail — the defense attorney can use the preliminary hearing transcript to impeach them in front of the jury. Witnesses who know their testimony is being locked in often give more careful, and sometimes less damaging, accounts.

Exposing inconsistencies early

A skilled cross-examination at the prelim can surface contradictions between what the witness says and what the police report, body camera footage, or other evidence shows. Those contradictions don't go away. They become part of the defense theory of the case — and they can shift how prosecutors evaluate the case's strength going into trial.

Learning the state's theory

Preliminary hearings are one of the clearest windows into how the prosecution plans to prove its case. Which witnesses will they lead with? What evidence are they emphasizing? Where are they soft? Defense attorneys who pay close attention to the preliminary hearing come out of it with a much clearer picture of what they'll be facing at trial — and what to attack.

Challenging unlawful searches

If the evidence against you came from a search that may have violated the Fourth Amendment, the preliminary hearing can surface the facts needed to support a suppression motion. Cross-examining the arresting officer about what they observed, what they searched, and why creates the evidentiary record for a motion to suppress — which, if successful, can gut the prosecution's case entirely. The Decision to Waive: Should You Ever Give Up the Prelim?

Prosecutors know the preliminary hearing is valuable to the defense, which is why they often offer something in exchange for waiving it. Common offers include a reduced charge, a favorable plea agreement, or a promise not to add additional counts. Sometimes these offers are genuinely worth considering. Often they are not.

The problem is that most defendants who waive their preliminary hearing do so without fully understanding what they're giving up. They're told the offer is time-sensitive, that things will go worse if they make the state go through the hearing, or that the prelim is just a formality. In most cases, none of that is entirely accurate.

There are legitimate reasons to waive in certain circumstances — when the evidence is overwhelming, when a deal on the table is genuinely exceptional, or when a preliminary hearing might cause more harm than good (for instance, when the cross-examination might solidify a witness's story rather than create doubt). But these are strategic decisions that require a thorough analysis of the specific facts, not a default choice made under pressure in the hallway outside the courtroom.

Never waive your preliminary hearing without first consulting with an experienced criminal defense attorney. The prosecution's offer will almost always still be available after the prelim — and the information you gain at the hearing could change your evaluation of that offer entirely.

How Preliminary Hearings Can Win Cases Before Trial

Outright dismissal at the preliminary hearing level is more common than people expect, particularly in cases that rest on weak witness identification, single-source testimony, or evidence obtained through a legally questionable stop or search.

Charges dismissed for lack of probable cause

If the state's witnesses can't establish the basic elements of the offense — or if their testimony contradicts what the charging document alleges — the judge can dismiss the case entirely at the conclusion of the hearing. The case doesn't go to trial. It's over. This happens most often in cases where the prosecution rushed to charge, where the key witness is unreliable under cross, or where the factual record simply doesn't support the charged offense.

Reduced charges after a strong cross

Even if the case is bound over, a strong preliminary hearing can shift the dynamic significantly. When a defense attorney exposes serious weaknesses in the state's key witnesses, prosecutors often recalibrate their assessment of the case — which can lead to charge reductions, better plea offers, or eventual dismissal as the trial date approaches.

Suppression of key evidence

The cross-examination record from a preliminary hearing can provide the factual basis for a subsequent motion to suppress. If evidence was obtained through an unlawful stop, search, or interrogation, winning a suppression motion can eliminate the prosecution's most important evidence — sometimes making the case impossible to prove. The preliminary hearing is often where the foundation for that motion is first built.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can charges be dropped at a preliminary hearing in Utah?

Yes. If the judge finds that the prosecution has not presented sufficient evidence to establish probable cause, they can dismiss the charges at the preliminary hearing. This is more common than many defendants realize — particularly when the state's case relies on a single witness, questionable forensic evidence, or an unlawful search.

What happens if I waive my preliminary hearing in Utah?

If you waive your preliminary hearing, your case is bound directly to district court for trial without the state being required to present evidence to establish probable cause. You give up the opportunity to cross-examine witnesses, lock in testimony, expose investigative weaknesses, and potentially have charges dismissed before trial.

Can I testify at my own preliminary hearing in Utah?

Technically yes, but in virtually every case the answer is no — you should not. The preliminary hearing is the state's burden to meet, not yours. Testifying creates a sworn record of your statements that can be used against you at trial. A good defense attorney will almost never advise their client to take the stand at a preliminary hearing.

How long does a Utah preliminary hearing take?

Most preliminary hearings in Utah last between 30 minutes and a few hours depending on the number of witnesses and the complexity of the case. In serious felony cases with multiple witnesses or contested evidence, hearings can extend over multiple days. The hearing must be held within a reasonable time after the initial appearance — typically within 30 days if you are in custody.
