01 Know Your Rights

If You've Been Arrested in Utah

These are not formalities. They are constitutional protections that can determine the outcome of your case. Know them. Use them.

1
You have the right to remain silent.

You are not required to answer questions beyond providing your name and basic identifying information. Do not try to explain yourself at the scene — that conversation happens with your attorney present.

"I am invoking my right to remain silent."
2
You have the right to an attorney.

If you cannot afford one, one will be appointed. But if you can choose your representation, choose wisely — public defenders carry enormous caseloads and limited time for each client.

3
You do not have to consent to a search.

Clearly state your refusal. This preserves your legal options even if the search proceeds anyway — an unlawful search can result in evidence being suppressed entirely.

"I do not consent to a search."
4
Anything you say can and will be used against you.

This is not a formality. Statements made at the scene — even attempts to cooperate or explain yourself — become evidence for the prosecution. Stay silent until you have legal counsel.

5
You have the right to know the charges against you.

Law enforcement must inform you of why you are being held. If you are not told, ask — and note the response.

Arrested or under investigation? The sooner you have legal representation, the better your outcome. Don't wait — every hour matters.

What to do immediately after a loved one is arrested

  1. Get their full name and date of birth ready for the inmate search — this is the fastest way to locate them in the system.
  2. Note the booking number if you have it — this speeds up the lookup and any calls to the facility.
  3. Check the bail amount listed — this determines your next steps for getting them released.
  4. Call a defense attorney before bail is posted if possible — an attorney may be able to challenge the bail amount at the initial appearance, potentially saving significant money.
04 Bail & Release

Understanding Bail in Utah

Bail is the amount of money required to secure release from custody while your case proceeds. In Utah, bail is set at the initial appearance — typically within 72 hours of arrest.

Factors That Influence Bail Amount
  • Severity of the charge
  • Prior criminal history
  • Flight risk assessment
  • Ties to the community — employment, family, length of residency
Utah Licensed Bail Bond Agents
  • Verify a bondsman's license before paying any fees.
  • insurance.utah.gov — Utah Insurance Department licensee lookup
Cash Bail

Pay the full bail amount directly to the court. Returned at case conclusion (minus fees) regardless of outcome.

Surety Bond — Bail Bondsman

Pay a non-refundable premium (typically 10% of bail) to a licensed bondsman who posts the full amount. Common when the full cash amount isn't available.

Own Recognizance (OR) Release

Released without bail based on a promise to appear. Requires a judge's approval — an attorney can advocate for this at your initial appearance.

Bail Reduction Motion

If bail is set unreasonably high, an attorney can file a motion to reduce it. This is one of the first and most impactful things Cliff can do for a new client.

An attorney can argue for lower bail or own recognizance release at your initial appearance. Having counsel in place before that hearing is critical.

05 Expungement

Can You Clear Your Record?

Utah's expungement law allows qualifying individuals to seal criminal records from public view — including arrests that didn't result in conviction, dismissed charges, and certain convictions after a waiting period.

Waiting Periods After Sentence Completion
Class B or C Misdemeanor3 years
Class A Misdemeanor5 years
Third Degree Felony7 years
Second Degree Felony10 years
First Degree FelonyNot eligible*

*Limited exceptions may apply. DUI expungements are subject to specific rules.

You May Be Eligible If
  • Your case was dismissed or you were acquitted
  • You completed your sentence and the waiting period has passed
  • You have not been convicted of certain serious offenses that disqualify
  • You have not been convicted of a subsequent offense
Utah BCI — Expungement Eligibility Tool
bci.utah.gov/expungement
The Expungement Process in Utah
Obtain a Certificate of Eligibility from the Utah Bureau of Criminal Identification (BCI).
File a Petition for Expungement in the appropriate court.
Notify relevant agencies — the prosecutor's office, arresting agency, and others as required.
Attend a hearing if any objections are filed by notified agencies.
Receive the court's Order of Expungement — your record is sealed from public view.

Expungement can open doors — employment, housing, professional licensing. Find out if you qualify. Learn more about Utah expungements →

07 What to Expect

What Happens After an Arrest.

A plain-language walkthrough of the Utah criminal process — from arrest to appeal.

1
Arrest & Booking

Fingerprints, photograph, personal property inventory. Held in county jail pending initial appearance. This is the moment to stay silent and request an attorney.

2
Initial Appearance

First court appearance, typically within 72 hours. Charges are read, bail is set. This is where having an attorney already in place matters most — bail arguments are made here, and the wrong outcome can keep a client in custody for weeks.

3
Preliminary Hearing (felonies)

The prosecution must show probable cause that a crime was committed and that you committed it. Defense can challenge evidence here — and a strong challenge can end a case before it starts.

4
Arraignment

Formal entry of your plea — guilty, not guilty, or no contest. In most cases, a not guilty plea is entered here while the case is still being evaluated.

5
Pre-Trial Motions

Defense files motions to suppress evidence, dismiss charges, or address procedural violations. Often the most critical phase — evidence obtained unlawfully can be excluded, fundamentally changing the prosecution's case.

→ See all practice areas
6
Plea Negotiations

Most cases resolve here. An experienced attorney can negotiate charge reductions, alternative sentencing, diversion programs, or dismissal — outcomes rarely available without skilled representation.

7
Trial

Jury or bench trial. The prosecution must prove guilt beyond a reasonable doubt — the highest legal standard. Defense challenges every element of the prosecution's case.

8
Sentencing

If convicted, the judge imposes the sentence. Defense can argue for leniency, alternatives to incarceration, probation, or treatment programs. The quality of advocacy at sentencing materially affects the outcome.

9
Appeals

If convicted, you have the right to appeal to the Utah Court of Appeals or the Utah Supreme Court. Appeals challenge legal errors that affected the fairness of the trial — not simply a re-examination of the facts.
